Changing Roles: Board and Staff under FPAS Mark II

Note

FPAS Mark II is not just a new forecasting framework‚Äîit represents a fundamental shift in how boards and staff work together. The move toward scenario-based analysis and a least-regrets mindset requires new habits, new incentives, and a different approach to collaboration.

Under FPAS Mark I, policy debates often gravitated around a single baseline. Under FPAS Mark II, the board focuses on strategic choices under uncertainty, while staff provide a structured set of realistic scenarios, risk assessments, and clear policy options. The quality of the policy discussion increasingly depends on the interaction between board and staff, not just on the models.

This lesson explains how roles evolve in this new environment‚Äîwhat is expected from the board, what is expected from staff, and how both sides can align around a common decision-making and learning process.

1. How the Board‚Äôs Role Changes under FPAS Mark II

In FPAS Mark II, the board‚Äôs primary task is to choose among strategies, not to fine-tune every technical assumption. This means:

  • Focusing on risk‚Äìreturn trade-offs across scenarios rather than minor differences in point forecasts.
  • Setting a clear risk appetite: what kinds of mistakes are more costly‚Äîbeing too tight or too loose? Under what conditions is a ‚Äúleast-regrets‚Äù strategy preferred?
  • Asking disciplined questions about narratives: what is driving the scenario? What would make it wrong?
  • Using scenarios as a tool for accountability over time: when the data come in, did we learn what we expected to learn?

2. How the Staff‚Äôs Role Changes under FPAS Mark II

For staff, FPAS Mark II is a move away from defending a single baseline toward becoming risk managers and storytellers of the data. Key changes include:

  • Designing a small, coherent set of Realistic, Relevant, and Robust scenarios that clearly map into alternative policy paths.
  • Quantifying uncertainty using fan charts, distributions, and risk metrics, rather than hiding uncertainty in judgmental adjustments.
  • Preparing decision-ready briefing materials that highlight choices, trade-offs, and trigger points‚Äînot just technical details.
  • Engaging in continuous post-mortem and ‚Äúpre-mortem‚Äù analysis: what could go wrong with the central scenario, and how would we recognize it quickly?

3. Building a Healthy Board‚ÄìStaff Interaction

FPAS Mark II works only if the interaction between board and staff is structured and respectful. Good practice includes:

  • A clear sequence of pre-briefs, technical meetings, and policy meetings, each with a distinct purpose.
  • Ground rules that protect open debate‚Äîstaff are encouraged to present risks and dissenting views without fear of blame.
  • Shared ownership of the learning process: both board and staff are invested in updating narratives and scenarios as new data arrive.
  • Using the FPAS Mark II cycle to align communications, internal incentives, and capacity-building around the same framework.
