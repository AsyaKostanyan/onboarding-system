1. Why Transparency Matters under FPAS Mark II

Transparency under FPAS Mark II plays a central role in anchoring expectations, reducing uncertainty, and strengthening policy transmission. By clearly explaining our framework and decisions, we lower the risk that markets misinterpret our actions and instead encourage them to internalize our risk assessment.

Rather than relying on speculation, well-designed transparency allows households, firms, and financial markets to understand:

  • how we see the balance of risks,
  • how uncertainty affects our decisions, and
  • how we are likely to respond as new information arrives.

FPAS Mark II integrates transparency into the entire policy cycle‚Äîforecasts, scenarios, meetings, communication, and post-round evaluations‚Äîso that credibility is built through openness, clarity, and disciplined reasoning.

2. Dimensions of Transparency

Transparency is multi-dimensional. Under FPAS Mark II, we emphasize several key dimensions that help the public understand not just the decision, but the framework and reasoning behind it:

  • Procedural transparency ‚Äî explains how decisions are made: the structure of meetings, the process for deliberation, and how information flows to the Board.
  • Analytical transparency ‚Äî explains the models, scenarios, and evidence we use: how we structure our forecasts and risk assessments.
  • Outcome transparency ‚Äî presents policy results clearly: what happened to inflation, output, and other objectives relative to our projections and scenarios.
  • Accountability transparency ‚Äî attributes reasoning and votes: how individual members or the Board as a whole explain their positions.

Together, these dimensions ensure that the public can see the full story: the framework, the analysis, the decision, and the ex post evaluation.

3. Transparency Tools at the Central Bank of Armenia

The Central Bank of Armenia uses a rich set of tools to put these principles into practice. These include:

  • Attributed minutes that summarize the discussion and perspectives.
  • Individual votes and justifications, showing how each member assessed the risks and trade-offs.
  • The Executive Statement, which provides a concise explanation of the decision and its rationale.
  • The Monetary Policy Report, which presents forecasts, scenarios, and the analytical backbone of the decision.
  • Scenario-based communication that highlights alternative paths, risks, and trade-offs.
  • Press conferences and analyst briefings, where Board members and staff answer questions and clarify how uncertainty is affecting decisions.

These tools work together to clarify the Board‚Äôs reasoning under uncertainty and to show how FPAS Mark II is applied in practice.

4. Scenario-Based Communication and Uncertainty

Scenario-based communication is a powerful way to enhance transparency. By showing alternative paths, risks, and trade-offs, we move away from a single deterministic forecast and toward a more realistic portrayal of the distribution of risks.

This approach:

  • Helps the public understand how different shocks could affect inflation, output, and financial conditions.
  • Makes clear which risks the central bank is most concerned about and how policy might respond if those risks materialize.
  • Strengthens public trust and policy credibility by being honest about what we know and what we do not know.

By explaining both our central scenario and the key alternative scenarios, we support better decision-making in the private sector and more effective transmission of monetary policy.

5. Transparency as a Foundation for Credibility

Finally, transparency is about more than documents and speeches. It is about how we earn credibility over time. By clearly explaining what we know, what we do not know, and how uncertainty affects our decisions, we invite the public to judge us on our reasoning and learning, not just on short-term outcomes.

FPAS Mark II embeds transparency into:

  • its analytical processes,
  • its communication strategy, and
  • its institutional culture.

In this way, transparency becomes foundational. Credibility is not assumed; it is earned through openness, clarity, and disciplined reasoning.
