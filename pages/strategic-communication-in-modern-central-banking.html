Strategic Communication in Modern Central Banking

Note

In modern central banking, communication is itself a policy tool. Under FPAS Mark II, strategic communication links scenarios, risk assessments, and decisions into a coherent story that helps anchor expectations and build credibility.

Communication is not about predicting every detail of the future. It is about explaining the reaction function, the least-regrets mindset, and the way the central bank learns from new information. Good communication makes the policy framework understandable and predictable without becoming mechanical or rigid.

This lesson focuses on how to design and deliver communication that supports the FPAS Mark II framework‚Äîinternally and externally.

1. Communication as a Policy Instrument

Communication affects expectations about inflation, interest rates, and the broader economy. Under FPAS Mark II:

  • Policy statements and press conferences are used to clarify the reaction function‚Äîhow the bank responds to different shocks and scenarios.
  • Scenario-based communication explains not just the baseline, but alternative paths and associated risks.
  • Forward guidance is designed to be state-contingent, not a fixed promise detached from incoming data.
  • Communication is aligned across documents (reports, minutes, speeches), reinforcing a coherent message.

2. Translating FPAS Mark II into Clear Narratives

One of the main challenges in strategic communication is translating complex models and scenarios into language that is clear, honest, and accessible. Good practice includes:

  • Structuring communication around a central narrative: What is happening? Why? What is the central bank doing about it?
  • Explaining the balance of risks, not just point forecasts.
  • Using visuals‚Äîfan charts, scenario plots, and simple tables‚Äîto show uncertainty and alternative outcomes.
  • Being transparent about what the bank does not know, while emphasizing its strategy for dealing with uncertainty.

3. Internal and External Communication

Strategic communication is both internal and external. Internally, it coordinates expectations and actions across departments; externally, it anchors expectations in markets and the wider public.

  • Internal communication ensures that staff understand the board‚Äôs objectives, reaction function, and risk preferences‚Äîso that analysis remains policy relevant.
  • External communication tailors messages to different audiences: financial markets, media, parliament, and the general public.
  • Consistent communication around scenarios and risks helps prevent misinterpretation of policy moves as ‚Äúsurprises‚Äù.
  • Feedback from stakeholders is used as an input into the learning process under FPAS Mark II.

4. Credibility, Transparency, and the Least-Regrets Mindset

Credibility depends on doing what you say and explaining clearly why you act the way you do. Under FPAS Mark II:

  • Communication explains not just the chosen policy rate, but the least-regrets logic behind it.
  • The bank is transparent about how new data will affect its views and what could trigger a change in stance.
  • Over time, consistent communication about scenarios, risks, and learning builds trust‚Äîeven when shocks are large and outcomes deviate from the central forecast.
